In the middle of 2019, I made a commitment to give away 10% of my (pre-tax) income for the rest of my life.

Speaking publicly about this kind of thing makes me extremely uncomfortable. Blabbing about one’s charity has always seemed horribly gauche to me, and whenever I see a billionaire put their name on a building, I assume one of two things:

  1. Imbuing a semi-permanent edifice with their name gives them a false sense of immortality and allows them to ignore the gnawing awareness of their inevitable death for a few minutes longer.
  2. The building represents a giant phallus and they're basically just doing the real estate version of swinging their big dick around in front of everyone.

The thought of talking about this kind of thing seeming like bragging, or like I’m trying to virtue signal about what a good person I am, makes me squirm1. Which is why I haven’t spoken about this publicly until now. I don’t even think most of my friends know.

1. Perhaps one day a plaid-sweatered therapist will pay for his beach house helping me figure out why I’m incredibly shy about divulging my good deeds, yet will share my most shameful and embarrassing stories to near-strangers with minimal prompting and wild abandon.

But I was finally convinced to swallow my discomfort and go public with this by an argument made by the Effective Altruism Foundation, whose approach to giving has heavily influenced me, and about whom you’ll hear more later.

They argue that one of the best things we can do is prosletyze our philanthropy, based on a simple argument. I’ll probably never start donating 20% of my income, and I certainly won’t ever start donating 100%. But I probably could convince nine other people in my same income bracket to make my same 10% commitment.

Based on that leverage, prosletyzing is probably the highest-impact thing I can do.

In this essay, I’m going to explain why I made this commitment, and why I think it’s something most people should do. I’ll also step through the mechanics of how I do it, and tell you about some of the unexpected benefits I’ve found from this practice.

And, of course, this is also a way for me to hold myself accountable: every year, I’ll update this page with the latest information on how much I donated, where I donated it to, and how much of a morally superior person I am for doing something so selfless2.

2. Just kidding about that last part, which I reaaalllly hope I didn’t have to spell out in this footnote for you.
